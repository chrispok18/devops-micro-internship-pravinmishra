# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

### Rules

* No "safe" answers.
* Must be your real belief (not copied from internet).
* Minimum 50 words.

**Hint:** What do you believe about career, money, learning, discipline, relationships, health, success, life, tech industry, etc. that most people don't agree with?

## Answer

Most people I know treat "finding your passion" as a prerequisite for starting. I believe the opposite: competence comes first, passion follows. I didn't love linux and coding when I started my Tech Journey I found it tedious and frustrating for the first year. What changed my feeling toward it wasn't a sudden spark of inspiration, it was getting good enough to build things that worked. The satisfaction came from competence, not the other way around. Most people around me wait to "feel ready" or "feel passionate" before committing real hours to a skill, and I think that's backwards. Discipline produces skill, skill produces results, and results produce the feeling people mistake for passion. Waiting to feel motivated before you start is why most people never start at all.

# Assignment 2. What are the top 3 objective truths you discovered through experimentation and results?

### Definition

Objective truths do not depend on opinions. They hold true regardless of how people feel.

Write each truth in this format:

**Truth:** (1 sentence)

**Evidence from my life:** (2–4 lines: what you tried + what happened)

## Truth #1

### Truth

Consistency at a low intensity beats intensity without consistency.

### Evidence from my life

I tried two approaches to learning AWS: one month of cramming 6-hour weekend sessions, and one month of 45 minutes every single day. The daily approach produced more retained knowledge and I actually finished the certification prep, while the weekend-cramming month left me burned out by week three with half the material forgotten. Same total hours, very different results.

---

## Truth #2

### Truth

Public accountability changes my execution rate more than private goal-setting does.

### Evidence from my life

I set the same body workout fitness goal privately twice and quit both times within three weeks. The third time, I posted my weekly progress on LinkedIn and medium. I finished the 12-week program. Nothing about the goal changed — only the fact that other people could see whether I followed through.

---

## Truth #3

### Truth

Sleep debt degrades output quality faster than it degrades output quantity.

### Evidence from my life

During a sprint week during my CSN bootcamp I kept my hours the same but cut sleep from 7 to 5 hours a night because i thought i was gooing to cover more with less sleep. I still produced the same number of commits, but I introduced twice the bugs and errors and had to trouble shoot for hours due to carelessness. The work looked done but it wasn't. My output were lacking because by brain required rest and was no optimally functioning

# Assignment 4. Have you ever cut corners (unethical / dishonest / shortcut behavior — not necessarily illegal)? If yes, how did it make you feel?

### Important

You don't need to write the full story.

Focus on the feeling:

* guilt
* fear
* shame
* stress
* regret
* numbness
* etc.

This is about self-awareness, not judgment.

### Answer Format

**Yes **

If Yes:

**What emotion did you feel?** (minimum 50–100 words)

## Answer

Early in one of my previous job at Hanypay, I was drowning on a deadline on some scripting operation and didn’t want to admit it. So I copied a chunk of a teammate’s script, dropped it into my report, and presented the whole thing as my own analysis. Nobody noticed. Not a single question about it from my Team leaser.That should have felt like a win. Instead it sat in my stomach for weeks. Every time that teammate spoke up in a meeting, this low-grade guilt would kick in. I started overcompensating  offering to help with random stuff, jumping in on things that had nothing to do with me and just to make the discomfort quieter.It was a cheap lesson, but it stuck: the real cost isn’t getting caught. It’s carrying it around yourself. That private awareness is a heavier, longer tax than just swallowing your pride and saying “I’m behind” ever would have been.
